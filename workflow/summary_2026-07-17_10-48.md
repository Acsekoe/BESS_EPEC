## New file: `model/central_planner_benchmark.py`

Cost-minimizing counterpart to the strategic EPEC. A single planner chooses BESS
power/energy at every node **and** the spot dispatch jointly, minimizing total
system resource cost:

    generation cost + curtailment cost + annualized storage CAPEX + degradation

subject to the same lower-level physics (nodal/system balance, PTDF line limits,
gen caps, SOC dynamics/periodicity), the same shared nodal connection limit, and
the same E/P envelope. It is one **convex QP** (no MPEC, no game, no
diagonalization) — the first-best siting/sizing benchmark. Ownership and market
prices are irrelevant to the planner: generation rent is a transfer, not a
resource cost, so it never appears. Single social discount rate (default 8%).

CLI: `python central_planner_benchmark.py --data <json> [--node-limit-mw N] [--wacc r] [--tag ...]`
Outputs to `output/central_planner[_tag]/`: summary.json, final_capacities.csv,
node_hour_prices.csv, storage_hour_operation.csv.

## Central-planner results — 9-bus

| | Planner @100 MW | Planner @200 MW |
|---|---:|---:|
| Total power | 326.6 MW | **326.6 MW** (identical) |
| Total energy | 1091 MWh | **1091 MWh** (identical) |
| System cost | 448,615.72 EUR/day | **448,615.72** (identical to the cent) |
| N8 (big PV) | 100.0 (capped) | 134.5 |
| N7 (CCGT) | 99.9 (capped) | 31.5 |

Interpretation: the planner's **optimal total storage is set by system economics**
(marginal system value = marginal storage cost), not by the connection cap. The
cap only reshuffles *where* capacity sits, at **zero cost penalty** here. At the
100 MW cap, N8 wanted >100 but was capped, so its excess spilled to the
electrically-similar N7 (pinning N7 at 100); relax to 200 and N8 takes its natural
134.5 MW while N7 collapses to its true optimum 31.5. N7/N8 are near-perfect
substitutes for the system -> total cost unchanged. **For the planner the node
limit is essentially cost-neutral.** The planner also spreads small positions
(7-14%) across all minor nodes for local savings the EPEC leaves unbuilt.

## EPEC result — 5-bus euro system, 200 MW cap (`output/epec/seidel_euro_200mw`)

Seidel, damping 0.7, portfolio4 investors, `--node-limit-mw 200`, `--max-iters 45`.
Did **not** converge (45-iter cap; same oscillation character, dipped to
dP=dE=0.036 at iter 40 then bounced; one transient "locally infeasible" solver
warning on a single MPEC that the retry logic absorbed). **This run is on the
5-bus euro system** (nodes N1-N5; generators G_Base/Mid/CCGT/Peak +
RES_Wind_N1, RES_PV_N3, RES_PV_N5).

| Investor | Type | Power MW | Energy MWh | Gen rent EUR/d | Settled EUR/d |
|---|---|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| I1 | merchant 8% | 96.9 | 235 | 0 | **-1,414** |
| I2 | merchant 12% | 51.7 | 111 | 0 | **-837** |
| I3 | wind port. 8% | 122.4 | 606 | 384,950 | 383,840 |
| I4 | solar port. 8% | 55.6 | 205 | 307,366 | 306,606 |

Per-node power (nonzero only): N1 = 141.7 (I1 90.7 / I2 51.0), N5 = 183.3
(I3 122.4 / I4 55.6 / merchants ~5), others ~0.

Three effects of the 200 MW cap on the EPEC:
1. **Cap stops binding** — no node reaches 200 (N5 tops at 183/200), **projection
   events = 0**. Doubling the cap fully relieves nodal-access scarcity; the access
   constraint is now slack, so there is no contested node.
2. **Portfolio investors polarize onto the PV node N5** — I3 (owns 20% PV_N5) and
   I4 (owns 80% PV_N5) pile 178 MW combined onto N5, far beyond what the old 100 MW
   cap allowed. Merchants retreat to N1 (cheap night wind).
3. **Merchants go negative** — I1 -1,414, I2 -837 EUR/day settled. With access no
   longer scarce they overbuild (optimistic-vs-settled gaps +4,833 / +2,419 show
   they *believed* they'd profit), spreads get competed away, settled profit turns
   negative. Loose access -> overinvestment -> losses, driven by optimistic dual
   selection.

## The 326.6 MW invariant

Total BESS is ~326.6 MW in **all three** settings:
- 9-bus central planner (100 and 200 MW cap): 326.6 MW
- 9-bus EPEC (portfolio4, 100 MW cap): 326.6 MW
- 5-bus euro EPEC (portfolio4, 200 MW cap): 326.6 MW

Euro and 9-bus share the same system-wide demand and generation totals, so the
aggregate storage the system supports (~326 MW) appears **pinned by the arbitrage
economics, largely independent of topology and node cap**. The cap and topology
move location/ownership and prices, not the aggregate quantity. This is a strong
comparison result: with 4 competing investors the EPEC matches the planner's
aggregate quantity (market power on *quantity* is competed away), but diverges on
*siting* (planner spreads for efficiency; strategic investors concentrate on
high-rent nodes) and on *prices* (planner flattens more: 9-bus LMP floor 43.9 vs
EPEC 30.3).

## Update 2026-07-17: LLP degradation and regularized MPEC test

### Corrected diagnosis of the four-investor 9-bus run

The saved `output/epec/seidel_portfolio4/summary.json` reports
`projection_event_count = 0`. The earlier attribution of the energy oscillation
to proportional projection was therefore incorrect. Power and aggregate energy
were essentially settled; the remaining `dE` came from spatial relocation among
near-price-equivalent nodes: I1 between N1/N7, I2 between N6/N8, and I4 between
N7/N8. The corresponding hourly LMP profiles differ by only about
`1e-5` to `3e-5 EUR/MWh`. This is economic indifference/nonuniqueness in siting,
not unstable aggregate investment. More damping may reduce visible movement but
cannot create a unique economic location.

The more material model issue is optimistic lower-level dual selection. The
investor MPECs selected favorable price vectors that were inconsistent with the
joint market reclear: I1/I2 embedded minimum LMPs were approximately zero,
I3/I4 minima were approximately 55 EUR/MWh, while the joint minimum was
30.28 EUR/MWh. Maximum MPEC-to-joint LMP differences were 24.7-30.3 EUR/MWh,
and optimistic-versus-settled profit gaps were about 4.6-59.1 kEUR/day. The final
joint reclear is consequently not yet a verified equilibrium.

### Implementation in `model/single_investor_mpec.py`

Only the standalone single-investor model was changed; the active
`epec_diagonalization.py` process had already loaded its code and was not affected.
That euro 200 MW EPEC run subsequently finished at its 45-iteration cap without
converging and with zero projection events.

The standalone MPEC now:

- includes physical degradation in the LLP objective as
  `0.5 * 15 * (P_ch + P_dis)`, i.e. 7.5 EUR/MWh on each storage leg;
- retains degradation in upper-level accounting, since the LLP dispatch cost and
  the investor's true operating cost serve different objectives and this is not
  double counting;
- applies a small convex dispatch regularizer (default `1e-4 EUR/MW2h`) to
  generation, charging/discharging, SOC, shedding, and net injection;
- includes the corresponding terms in stationarity and Wolfe strong duality;
- uses the same degradation and regularization rule in the reference market QP;
- exposes the coefficient through `--dispatch-regularization`.

This is the appropriate formulation while the ISO directly dispatches storage.
If strategic storage bids later become upper-level variables, the LLP should use
the bid prices and the upper-level profit should subtract true degradation cost.

Investment power is insensitive to this range, while the dual-price discrepancy
persists. Quadratic primal regularization is therefore useful as a neutral
dispatch tie-break but is not a sufficient dual-price selection rule.

### Revised priority

Keep LLP degradation and the small primal regularizer. Before relying on another
four-investor EPEC result, implement one common, investor-neutral price-selection
rule consistently in every MPEC and in the joint settlement (for example, a
separate minimum-norm-dual pricing stage or another explicitly justified market
rule). Then require near-zero investor-versus-settlement LMP gaps and check
unilateral regret. Damping and the access auction remain separate questions and
do not resolve optimistic dual selection.
