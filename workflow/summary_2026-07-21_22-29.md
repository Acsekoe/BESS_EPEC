## Current objective

Continue the IEEE-9-bus four-investor EPEC study with a common nodal BESS
access price replacing the solve-order-dependent shared-limit projection. The
immediate question is whether the price mechanism can produce a feasible,
stable variational-equilibrium candidate and reveal investor-specific access
WTP at the binding node. The IEEE-9-only scope from the previous handoff still
stands.

## Starting point

The prior session had already implemented price mode:

- each investor pays `capacity_price[n] * X_power[n]`;
- the private rival-headroom bound is removed in price mode;
- a projected price update raises the common price when aggregate nodal
  capacity exceeds 100 MW and lowers it when the node is slack;
- settlement subtracts access-price payments;
- projection mode remains the default and is unchanged.

The first smoke-test interpretation was wrong because the terminal diagnostic
used `abs(total - limit)`: an empty node appeared as a 100 MW overload.

## Economic interpretation retained

- Electricity LMP `lambda[n,t]` is the lower-level nodal-balance dual.
- Access price `lambda_cap[n]` is intended to be the multiplier of the shared
  nodal BESS limit, but computationally it is reconstructed by projected price
  iteration rather than returned directly by an embedded shared constraint.
- The 100 MW limit is per node, not system-wide. Node overload is deliberately
  allowed during price discovery and must disappear at equilibrium.
- At a binding-node equilibrium, an active interior investor's marginal access
  WTP equals the common access price; an inactive investor's marginal WTP is at
  most that price. Total portfolio profit is not WTP.
- The runs suggest I4 has the highest N8 access valuation, then I3, while I1/I2
  drop out at much lower prices. This remains conditional evidence, not an
  equilibrium WTP curve.
- Access pricing does not remove the optimistic electricity-dual selection
  caveat documented in `project_context.md`.

## Big-M/MILP discussion

- Big-M can linearize explicit KKT complementarity after replacing the current
  strong-duality equality, but it cannot exactly linearize arbitrary products
  of two continuous variables in the upper objective.
- An MILP would additionally require revenue reformulation and/or discrete
  MW/MWh investment blocks. It would add many binaries and require defensible,
  tight dual bounds.
- A globally solved investor MILP would not fix the outer access-price or EPEC
  cycle. Treat this only as a possible later reduced-case robustness check, not
  the immediate solution.

## Recommended next algorithmic change

Do not continue the same fixed-step, one-Seidel-sweep-per-price-update run or
only tune the step again. Separate the two processes:

1. Hold all nodal access prices fixed and iterate investor best responses until
   the inner investor game stabilizes, or explicitly detect a fixed-price
   cycle.
2. Only then update nodal prices using averaged aggregate excess demand rather
   than the last single sweep.
3. Use a diminishing or otherwise stabilized outer price step after the inner
   behavior is understood.
4. Record separate inner and outer iteration counters and keep the existing
   checkpoint/resume diagnostics.

If the fixed-price inner game itself cycles, that is evidence that either a
pure-strategy equilibrium is absent under the current optimistic best-response
selection or the NLP is switching between local branches. Possible follow-ups
are proximal/inertial best responses, ergodic averaging, or an explicit auction
allocation/rationing rule for a discontinuous demand jump that straddles the
100 MW supply.

Also improve failed-solve diagnostics before relying on longer CPU limits:
record Ipopt solver status/message and reproduce any persistent failed best
response with `tee=True`, checking constraint violations and dual-bound
activity. More CPU time cannot repair genuine infeasibility.
