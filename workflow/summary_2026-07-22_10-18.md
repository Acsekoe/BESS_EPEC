## What the access-price experiment attempted

The experimental mechanism replaced the private rival-headroom constraint with
one common access price per node. Investor `i` paid

```text
lambda_cap[n] * X_power[i,n]
```

and aggregate nodal demand updated the price through

```text
lambda_cap_new[n]
  = max(0, lambda_cap_old[n] + step * (sum_i X_power[i,n] - node_limit[n])).
```

Two update timings were tested:

1. **Sweep update:** hold prices fixed for a complete Seidel sweep, then update
   prices once after I1--I4 have responded.
2. **Investor update:** update prices after every investor response, so I2 sees
   the price created after I1, I3 sees the price after I2, and so on.

The price-complementarity residual measured whether another projected price
step would still move the price. It is zero when a node is either binding at a
possibly positive price or slack at a zero price. An overloaded node, or a slack
node with a positive price, has a positive residual measured in MW.

## Why it did not work

The main problem was discontinuous and spatially coupled investor best responses,
not merely an unsuitable step size or insufficient solver time.

- Aggregate N8 demand repeatedly jumped across the 100 MW supply quantity. A
  typical pattern was approximately 62--79 MW on the low branch and 124--132 MW
  on the high branch. There was no observed price at which the selected pure
  best responses continuously approached exactly 100 MW.
- Capacity also switched among N3, N8, and N9. Consequently, the relevant demand
  mapping was multidimensional rather than an independent smooth demand curve at
  each node.
- With one price update per sweep, the price reacted to a transient investor
  state after only one Seidel pass, not to a settled fixed-price investor game.
- Updating after every investor made the price response faster and changed the
  trajectory, but did not remove the underlying capacity jump. It also made the
  transient path explicitly dependent on investor solve order.
- Reducing the sweep-level step from 1.0 to 0.25 slowed the dynamics but did not
  remove the cycle. Using 0.0625 per investor update, approximately one quarter
  of 0.25, also failed to stabilize the system.

Key run evidence:

- `seidel_ieee9_congestion_100_price_step1_maxiter20`: iteration 20 ended with
  N8 at 132.85 MW, a 32.85 MW overload, and a price of 92.00 EUR/MW/day.
- `seidel_ieee9_congestion_100_price_step025`: iteration 20 ended with N8 at
  132.10 MW, a 32.10 MW overload, and a price of 127.29 EUR/MW/day.
- `seidel_ieee9_congestion_100_price_step025_iter21_40`: the resumed run
  confirmed a multi-node cycle and ended at iteration 40 with N8 at 131.26 MW,
  a 31.26 MW overload, and a price of 43.60 EUR/MW/day.
- `seidel_ieee9_congestion_100_price_investor_step00625_manual`: investor-level
  updates initially moved N8 from severe overload to slack, but it jumped back
  to the high branch. The user stopped the run after iteration 19 with N8 at
  132.06 MW, a price of 74.49 EUR/MW/day, and a 32.06 MW overload/residual. All
  completed MPEC solves in this run were optimal, strongly indicating an
  algorithmic/branch-selection problem rather than an Ipopt failure.

Therefore, none of the price-mode capacities, prices, or profits are equilibrium
results. They are diagnostic histories only.

## Proposed pay-as-bid nodal-access auction

The auction is a new market design, not another numerical solver for the former
common-price equilibrium. Each investor submits one sealed bid block per node:

```text
quantity_bid[i,n]  [MW]
price_bid[i,n]     [EUR/MW/day]
```

The auctioneer independently clears each node by descending bid price:

1. Accept the highest-priced bid quantities first.
2. Continue until the fixed nodal connection limit is exhausted.
3. Partially accept the marginal bid when its requested quantity exceeds the
   remaining capacity.
4. Ration equal-price marginal bids pro rata using an explicit deterministic
   tie rule.
5. Never allocate more than the nodal limit.

Define awarded power separately from requested power:

```text
X_power_awarded[i,n] <= quantity_bid[i,n]
sum_i X_power_awarded[i,n] <= node_limit[n].
```

Only awarded power enters the physical spot-market model and investment CAPEX.
Energy investment must satisfy the existing duration envelope against awarded
power:

```text
2 * X_power_awarded[i,n]
  <= X_energy[i,n]
  <= 8 * X_power_awarded[i,n].
```

Under pay-as-bid settlement, each investor pays

```text
sum_n price_bid[i,n] * X_power_awarded[i,n].
```

The investor objective remains spot-storage revenue plus owned-generator rent,
minus degradation, CAPEX, and the auction payment.

## Simple implementation path

1. **Specify the institution first.** Confirm one bid block per investor-node,
   EUR/MW/day units, bid and quantity caps, partial acceptance, pro-rata ties,
   and treatment of undersubscribed nodes.
2. **Create one small standalone clearing module.** Adapt the deterministic
   merit-order logic from `model/old/auction.py`; do not import or revive the
   outdated two-investor driver wholesale.
3. **Test clearing independently.** Cover full acceptance, partial marginal
   acceptance, equal-price rationing, undersubscription, zero bids, and the
   exact 100 MW feasibility condition.
4. **Validate the physical chain with fixed bids.** Clear bids, pass awarded MW
   into the existing spot-market model, optimize/validate MWh, and calculate
   pay-as-bid payments and profits.
5. **Add strategic bid best responses only after fixed-bid clearing works.** Hold
   rival bids fixed, let one investor search over its quantity/price bid, clear
   the auction, and evaluate the resulting market profit. Then wrap these bid
   responses in Jacobi or Seidel updates.
6. **Prefer a small discrete candidate set initially.** Merit-order allocation
   is discontinuous, and continuous bid price multiplied by awarded quantity is
   bilinear. A finite set of economically relevant bid prices/quantity blocks or
   unilateral-deviation enumeration keeps the code more transparent than an
   immediate large Big-M formulation.
7. **Verify the final candidate.** Check hard nodal feasibility, auction payment
   consistency, bid-order/tie behavior, spot-market settlement, and profitable
   unilateral bid deviations. Report solve-order dependence if Jacobi and
   Seidel produce different bid profiles.

Important: the auction guarantees feasible allocation at every clearing, but it
does not itself guarantee that strategic bids converge to a Nash equilibrium.
Its advantage is that the rationing rule is explicit and economically
interpretable when aggregate demand jumps over the available 100 MW.
