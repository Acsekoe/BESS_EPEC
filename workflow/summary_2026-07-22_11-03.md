# Handoff Summary — Pay-as-Bid Nodal-Access Auction: First Implementation

Date: 2026-07-22 11:03 (Europe/Vienna)

## Current objective

Implement the pay-as-bid nodal grid-access auction proposed in the previous
handoff (`summary_2026-07-22_10-18.md`) as a new market design that replaces the
failed nodal-price tatonnement. The auction clears each node's fixed connection
limit as a hard constraint, so allocation is always feasible; strategic bidding
convergence is not guaranteed and is to be reported honestly.

## Agreed specification (this session)

Four decisions were fixed with the user before coding:

1. **One bid block per investor per node** — a single `(quantity, price)` pair.
2. **Payment is a pure transfer** — subtracted from investor profit, netted out
   of any social-cost comparison with the central planner (which levies no
   access charge).
3. **Go straight to strategic bidding** (not a fixed-bid deliverable first).
4. **Energy sizing unchanged** — `2*awarded <= X_energy <= 8*awarded`, optimized
   inside the MPEC.

Follow-up decisions: bids stay one pair per node across all nodes; **displacement
is allowed** (an investor may outbid rivals to both secure its cap and push rival
BESS out of the market). Multi-node search method left to implementation:
**coordinate-wise greedy over contested nodes**.

## Changed / added files

- **`model/nodal_access_auction.py`** (new, maintained). Sealed pay-as-bid
  clearing adapted from `model/old/auction.py`: one block per investor-node,
  independent per-node descending-price merit order, equal-price pro-rata
  rationing, partial marginal fill, hard `awarded <= node_limit`. Returns awarded
  MW per (investor, node), pay-as-bid payments, and node utilization.
- **`model/single_investor_mpec.py`** (modified, additive). New optional
  parameter `investor_power_cap_mw`. When passed, it overrides the per-node
  `invest_limit` with an explicit awarded cap, **decoupling the investor's
  connection right from the physical rival fleet** in `rival_power_mw` (still
  clipped into headroom for feasibility). Defaults preserve the existing
  rival-headroom behaviour, so the baseline EPEC is unchanged.
- **`model/epec_auction.py`** (new). Strategic pay-as-bid EPEC by
  diagonalization:
  - `residual_candidates()` — finite candidate bids per node from the
    residual-supply staircase: claim free leftover at price ~0, or bid just above
    each rival price level to jump the queue (displacement).
  - `best_response()` — coordinate-wise greedy over **contested** nodes only
    (nodes where extra capacity costs a positive price); other nodes just claim
    the free residual and let the MPEC decide the build. Each candidate: clear
    auction -> cap the MPEC at the awarded MW -> net profit = MPEC market profit
    minus pay-as-bid payment.
  - `run_auction_epec()` — Gauss-Jacobi / Gauss-Seidel sweeps, per-iteration hard
    feasibility assertion, convergence on max per-node MW move, honest
    non-convergence reporting. Minimal JSON export.

## Two explicit v1 modelling choices

- **(S1) No pay-for-idle foreclosure.** An investor's emitted bid quantity equals
  the MW it actually builds, so it never pays to hold idle connection rights just
  to exclude rivals. Displacement still happens via price ranking (queue-jumping
  secures the investor's own block ahead of rivals).
- **(S2) Rival consistency (single pass).** Rival physical presence in an
  investor's MPEC is `min(installed, awarded)`, computed by clearing with the
  active investor's warm-start build as its provisional bid quantity. A true
  two-pass reconciliation was dropped for solve cost (see below).

## Verification completed

- `py_compile` passes for `nodal_access_auction.py`, `epec_auction.py`,
  `single_investor_mpec.py`.
- **Clearing**: a throwaway test covering full acceptance, partial marginal fill,
  equal-price pro-rata rationing, undersubscription, zero/zero-price bids,
  exact-limit feasibility, multi-node independence, unknown-node rejection, and
  the `awarded_for` helper — **all pass**.
- **Capped MPEC**: builds and solves in ~1 s with an explicit cap
  (`N8 = 60 MW`), respects the cap (built 53.9 MW), and optimally builds below
  the cap when unprofitable.
- **Candidate generator**: for I1 at N8 with a rival bidding 40 MW @ 25
  EUR/MW/day, produces exactly {build 0; claim-free 60 MW @ ~0; displace-rival
  100 MW @ 25.01} — economically correct.

## Key observations

1. **Feasibility works as intended.** Every clearing respects the 100 MW nodal
   limit by construction; the driver additionally asserts it each iteration. This
   is the concrete improvement over the tatonnement, which stalled ~32 MW over
   the limit.

2. **Solve cost is the binding constraint.** Representative full-9-node MPEC solve
   times:
   - all-nodes cap = 100, no rivals: **2.7 s**, single investor builds ~56 MW;
   - all-nodes cap = 100, rival I2 = 40 MW @ N8: **41.9 s**, single investor
     builds **198.9 MW** (N3 68.7, N8 60, N9 70.2).
   The expensive, rival-present case is exactly what best-response evaluates
   repeatedly. The solve budget was cut hard (1-pass consistency,
   contested-nodes-only sweep, warm-start chaining) to ~3-5 solves per
   best-response, but a full four-investor run is still projected at roughly
   **1-3 hours** and currently has **no checkpoint/resume**.

3. **Some solves do not converge in time.** In the one-investor best-response
   smoke run, individual capped MPEC solves hit the Ipopt `max_cpu_time`
   (`Maximum CPU Time Exceeded`, termination `maxIterations`) at 60 s. These
   candidates are currently treated as failed and skipped, which can silently
   distort the best-response. This must be addressed (larger time budget, better
   warm start/scaling, or a bound tightening) before results are trusted.

4. **Optimistic-MPEC inflation carries into bids.** A single merchant investor
   wanting ~199 MW once a rival appears is driven by the optimistic dual prices
   that `project_context.md` already flags. Strategic bids inherit this: investors
   will bid aggressively on capacity that joint settlement may show as
   loss-making. Auction output therefore needs the **same optimistic-vs-joint-
   settlement disclosure** as the baseline EPEC; do not report optimistic net
   profits as realised rents.

## Next steps

1. **Fix solve reliability first.** Diagnose the >60 s / non-converging capped
   MPEC (dual-bound scaling, warm start from the reference dispatch, or Ipopt
   options). A best-response built on timed-out solves is not trustworthy.
2. **Add checkpoint/resume** to `epec_auction.py` (mirror
   `epec_diagonalization.py`) before any multi-hour run.
3. **Run a small validation first**: 2 investors, few iterations, to confirm the
   loop produces feasible, economically sensible bidding and to observe whether
   it converges or cycles. Only then attempt the four-investor run.
4. **Add joint-settlement re-clear** and report optimistic MPEC net profit
   alongside joint-settlement net profit per investor, plus access payments as
   transfers.
5. **Planner comparison** at the same 100 MW nodal limit: aggregate MW/MWh,
   siting, and social cost with auction payments netted out.

## Caveats / open questions

- The auction guarantees feasibility, **not** convergence: merit-order allocation
  is discontinuous, so the bidding game can still cycle. A non-converged run is a
  feasible iterate, never an equilibrium.
- (S1)/(S2) are deliberate simplifications; pay-for-idle foreclosure and a full
  two-pass rival reconciliation are documented extensions, not yet implemented.
- No commit/push performed. The one-investor best-response smoke run was still
  in progress at the time of writing (slow due to 60 s solve caps); its numeric
  result is not yet recorded here.
